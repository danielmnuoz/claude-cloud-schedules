Saturday, April 19, 2026

US Markets & Finance

  • Fresh record close Friday (Apr 18): The Dow rose 869 pts (+1.79%) to 49,447; the S&P 500 gained ~1.2% (its 8th record close of 2026); the Nasdaq added ~1.5%, extending its winning streak to 12 consecutive sessions.
  • Catalyst: Iran opening the Strait of Hormuz to non-Iranian commercial shipping triggered broad risk-on sentiment, lifting equities and hammering energy prices. Semiconductor and large-cap tech (XLK) led gains.
  • Notable movers: Energy stocks fell sharply on oil's 10%+ plunge; defense names gave back recent gains on ceasefire optimism; semiconductors rallied on geopolitical de-escalation hopes.
  • Fed / monetary policy: The Fed held rates steady at 3.5–3.75% at its March meeting — its second consecutive hold. PCE and Core PCE inflation for 2026 were revised up to 2.7% (from 2.4–2.5% in December projections), complicating the path to cuts. Policymakers still pencil in one cut this year.
  • Economic data: The labor market remains relatively tight while inflation stays stubbornly above target, leaving the Fed in a holding pattern ahead of its April 28–29 meeting where no action is expected.

International Markets & Geopolitics

  • Iran War — Day 50 (critical juncture): The US-Israel conflict with Iran (launched Feb 28) sits at an inflection point. A two-week ceasefire agreed April 7–8 expires April 22. Iran threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz again on April 18, citing US "breaches of trust," even as indirect talks on a ceasefire extension continue. US intelligence assesses roughly half of Iran's missile launchers remain intact, many buried underground.
  • Lebanon ceasefire: A separate Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire took effect April 16, following fighting that killed 2,000+ civilians and militants. Negotiations are ongoing between Israel and Lebanon.
  • Oil: Brent crude trades near $100/barrel — sharply down from recent highs after Hormuz opening news, but supply-risk premium remains elevated given the ceasefire's April 22 expiry.
  • Gold: Spot gold at ~$4,850/oz, up over 41% year-over-year, driven by safe-haven demand during the conflict. Gold pulled back slightly as ceasefire optimism lifted risk appetite.
  • US-China trade: Bilateral trade has fallen ~30% under tariff escalation, with the US replacing roughly two-thirds of the gap via other trading partners. The Trump-Xi summit was rescheduled from April to May 14–15 due to the Iran war; expectations are for narrow agricultural/commercial agreements rather than a grand bargain.
  • Syria sanctions: The US revoked its comprehensive Syria sanctions program, though Assad-specific, human rights, and Captagon-related sanctions remain in force.
  • Dollar: The USD index firmed +0.38% Friday; a stronger dollar modestly weighed on gold and EM currencies.
  • Global growth: The IMF projects global growth at ~3.1% for 2026, with the US slowing to ~1.8%, Europe at ~1.1%, and China holding near 5%.

Tech & Business

  • Q1 2026 tech layoffs — 78,557 cut: Nearly 80,000 tech workers lost jobs in Q1, with ~48% of positions eliminated explicitly attributed to AI-driven automation and workflow changes. April is on pace to be the worst month of the year.
  • Oracle: Cut an estimated 20,000–30,000 employees in early April to redeploy capital into AI data center expansion — savings could reach ~$10B, earmarked for GPU clusters and infrastructure buildout.
  • Amazon: Eliminated 16,000 jobs globally despite committing to massive AI infrastructure spending as part of the industry's $650B combined capex cycle.
  • Block (Square/Cash App): CEO Jack Dorsey cut 4,000 roles (~40% of global headcount) in March, citing AI tools' expanding capability to handle tasks previously requiring human workers.
  • AI capex supercycle: Amazon, Meta, Google, and Microsoft plan a combined ~$650B in AI infrastructure investment in 2026 — the largest coordinated capital deployment in tech history, targeting data centers, chips, networking, and energy.
  • AI-washing debate: OpenAI CEO Sam Altman acknowledged publicly that some companies are blaming AI for layoffs that would have occurred regardless, calling it "AI washing" of normal cost-cutting cycles.
