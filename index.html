Iran War — Day 61 (Developing)

  • President Trump extended the US-Iran ceasefire indefinitely (following the initial two-week truce announced April 7–8), but peace negotiations remain stalled: Iran refuses to return to the table until the US naval blockade of Iranian ports is lifted, while Washington insists on a comprehensive deal covering Iran's nuclear program, missile program, and regional proxies.
  • The Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed, with traffic reduced to near zero — the passage normally accounts for roughly 20% of global energy flows, and the shutdown is now in its seventh week, driving sustained oil price pressure.
  • An Israel-Lebanon truce was extended three weeks following White House diplomacy on April 23–24, but Israeli forces and Hezbollah fighters continued to exchange fire despite the extension.
  • Trajectory: A diplomatic breakthrough remains the biggest single upside catalyst for energy markets; a breakdown and re-escalation is the primary tail risk heading into May, with Brent potentially surging past $110 if the ceasefire collapses.

Trade Policy & Tariffs

  • The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling barred use of IEEPA to impose tariffs, forcing the administration to pivot; Trump subsequently imposed a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, valid for 150 days (through July 24, 2026). The government is processing ~$166B in refunds to businesses that paid the now-invalid IEEPA tariffs.
  • Sector-specific levies remain: 50% tariff on metals (articles entirely of aluminum, steel, or copper) and 25% on advanced semiconductor imports. The average effective US tariff rate is now ~11.8%.
  • The EU is accelerating free trade talks with Canada, Japan, and South Korea as a hedge against US trade policy volatility; the EU-Mercosur FTA, if finalized, would create the world's largest free-trading area.

Tech & Business

Layoffs & Restructuring

  • Meta announced 8,000 layoffs (10% headcount reduction) to begin May 20, even as it ramps AI capex to $115–135B in 2026 — a 67–97% increase year-over-year. CEO Zuckerberg framed the cuts as necessary to fund the AI buildout.
  • Oracle cut at least 10,000 employees in April (~6% of its workforce), with restructuring expected to reach up to 30,000 total; the moves are tied to AI-driven workflow automation.
  • Snap axed 1,000 jobs (16% of its workforce) on April 15, with the company stating bluntly that 65% of its code is now AI-generated — the most direct public admission yet of AI displacing software engineers.
  • Industry-wide, tech laid off ~78,557 workers in Q1 2026, with nearly 48% of cuts explicitly attributed to AI and automation — the first quarter where AI displacement has overtaken other restructuring rationales as the leading cause.

AI Industry Developments

  • Tonight's Mag 7 earnings will function as a sector-wide referendum on AI ROI: the four companies collectively plan $600–645B in AI capex in 2026, and investors demand evidence that cloud revenue acceleration and AI product adoption justify the spend.
  • An OpenAI revenue report circulating Tuesday showed targets being missed, sending chip stocks lower and raising questions about whether AI monetization is keeping pace with infrastructure investment — a key narrative heading into tonight's reports.
  • The AI-to-software displacement trend is accelerating: Snap's 65%-AI-code admission and Mag 7 capex plans signal that the "year of AI efficiency" is now in full effect across Big Tech.
